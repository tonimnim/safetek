A client came to us with a $14,200 monthly AWS bill and a feature shipped in 2022 that they couldn't quite kill. Eight weeks later they were paying $6,800. They didn't lose any features. Latency improved. Here's how.

## Step 1 — get the bill into a spreadsheet

This sounds dumb. It's not. The AWS console is optimized for upselling. You need every line item, in one place, sorted by spend, with last-month comparisons. Cost Explorer's CSV export plus thirty minutes in a spreadsheet beats every dashboard tool we've tried.

Once it's there, the work is mechanical:

1. Anything over $200/month gets a name on it.
2. The named owner has 48 hours to justify it.
3. Anything unjustified or unowned is a candidate for deletion.

About 30% of their bill failed step 2.

## Step 2 — kill the zombies

Top categories of waste, in order:

- **Idle EC2.** Three instances running staging environments nobody had touched in months. Snapshot, stop, document, schedule deletion in 30 days.
- **NAT Gateway egress.** A misrouted internal service was sending traffic out to the internet and back in. $1,800/month, fixed in an afternoon by a VPC endpoint.
- **Old EBS snapshots.** Years of automated snapshots, none of them tied to a retention policy. We didn't delete them; we tagged the lot for cold storage. ~$400/month saved.
- **CloudWatch logs.** Default retention was "forever." Almost nothing actually needed more than 30 days. ~$300/month.

## Step 3 — right-size the survivors

Anything that survived step 2 got an honest look at utilization. Most of their production EC2 was running on burstable instances at 8% CPU. Some were genuinely undersized. A few we replaced with Fargate where the load was bursty.

Right-sizing isn't sexy and the wins per change are small, but added up across a fleet it's the third-biggest line item we touched.

## Step 4 — commit, but only to what's stable

Reserved Instances and Savings Plans are great if your workload is predictable, terrible if it isn't. We modeled their last 12 months of usage, identified the floor — the load they always carry — and bought 3-year Compute Savings Plans for that floor only. About 35% of compute spend, locked in at a 40% discount.

The rest stayed on-demand. We didn't try to commit to growth they hadn't booked.

## What we didn't do

We didn't migrate frameworks. We didn't change their cloud provider. We didn't restructure their team. We didn't sell them an observability platform.

The savings came from boring blocking-and-tackling work that any engineer could do, but that nobody had time to do.
